]]>http://localhost:2368/product-management-is-art-not-science/619fa754c890f0063f5cfc41Thu, 25 Nov 2021 15:10:47 GMTProduct management is a hot term these days, it generates a lot of intrigue and debate. The volume of literature available on the subject has risen exponentially in recent years. An entire industry has cropped up to consult entrepreneurs and executives. Long story short, PM is hot these days.  One only has to look at the beeline B-School grads are making (which is a very good marker for the industry in vogue). The naysayers would say PM is huff-n-puff, but the proof is in the pudding.  Technology is truly eating the world, but the best technology is still useless if no-one really needs it.  This is evidenced by Marty Cagan at the beginning of his book, when he recounts how HP somehow conspired to fail with a team of the world's best engineers at the time.  The reason? No one wanted what they had built.  We now understand, how absolutely vital the PM's role is.  We tend to appreciate less the fact that despite such great responsibilities, the humble PM is not granted any great powers, in fact he has to win over everyone to accomplish anything. To succeed, he has to be astute with people, read the market very well and know how to execute,execute, execute and execute. Sounds very much like the E-word.  Cagan, a PM veteran who has launched some of the world's most admired products, says very clearly that to win you need to orient and think collaboration from the get-go. Innovation and ideas don't come from focus groups, but  from everyone who is involved in building for, or talking to, the customers  (which means pretty much everyone in a reasonably small company). This collaborative innovative-thinking-from-everyone approach is echoed by some of the world's best entrepreneurs.

Cagan, also puts into perspective, the two elephants in the room LEAN and AGILE, used correctly these frameworks can absolutely supercharge innovation, the rub? the way they are thought about and implemented, is inherently flawed in most companies.
